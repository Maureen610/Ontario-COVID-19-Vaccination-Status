1 INTRODUCTION

According to the World Health Organization ((WHO2024aimpact?)), COVID-19 has resulted in substantial global loss of life. In Canada, approximately 4,949,441 cases and 59,139 deaths had been reported as of April 16, 2024 (Cascella et al. 2023). At the provincial level, Ontario recorded about 1,609,170 cases and 16,404 deaths as of July 26, 2024 (Little 2020). Vaccination is the most effective COVID-19 intervention (Rees et al. (2022)); analyzing uptake by age, sex, geography, and time helps identify inequities and guide targeted strategies.

This report presents the first stage of analysis for the Ontario vaccination dataset. The focus is on importing the data, cleaning and preparing it for analysis, and producing descriptive statistics and visualizations that summarize the key features of the dataset.

6 DATA WRANGLING

6.1 Convert Data Types

data <- vaccine_data |>
  mutate(
    public_health_unit = as.factor(public_health_unit),
    gender = as.factor(gender),

      age_group,
      levels = c("0 to 4", "5 to 11", "12 to 17", "18 to 29", "30 to 39",

      ordered = TRUE
    ),
    vaccination_status = factor(
      vaccination_status,

6.2 Create Derived Variables

data <- data |>

      number_of_individuals_vaccinated / population,

    month = month(week_start_date, label = TRUE, abbr = TRUE)
  )

11 Define the Binary Outcome

binary_long <- clean |>
  filter(vaccination_status == "At least one dose") |>
  transmute(
    public_health_unit,
    gender,
    age_group,
    week_start_date,
    population,
    n_vaccinated   = number_of_individuals_vaccinated,
    n_unvaccinated = pmax(population - number_of_individuals_vaccinated, 0)
  )

# Sanity check
binary_long |>
  summarise(across(c(n_vaccinated, n_unvaccinated), sum))
  • A binary vaccination outcome was constructed by defining vaccination as receipt of at least one dose. For each week and demographic stratum, the number of vaccinated and unvaccinated individuals was calculated using population denominators. This binary structure enabled population‑based analyses of vaccination uptake and supported subsequent regression modeling. Therefore, the outcome is vaccinated with at least one dose (1) vs. not vaccinated (0), which aligns with Guay et al. (Guay et al. 2022).

12 Collapse to Person-Level Summary per Stratum

uptake <- binary_long |>
  group_by(public_health_unit, gender, age_group) |>
  summarise(
    population     = max(population,     na.rm = TRUE),
    n_vaccinated   = max(n_vaccinated,   na.rm = TRUE),
    .groups = "drop"
  ) |>
  mutate(
    n_unvaccinated = pmax(population - n_vaccinated, 0),
    uptake_rate    = n_vaccinated / population
  )

uptake
  • Weekly vaccination records were collapsed to a single person‑level summary for each combination of public health unit, gender, and age group. Population size and cumulative vaccination counts were retained using maximum values across time to avoid double‑counting. Vaccination uptake rates were then calculated as the proportion of individuals who received at least one dose within each stratum.

Reason. Weekly counts are cumulative, so the maximum across weeks is the total uptake by Dec 3, 2023. This collapses ~424,000 weekly rows into one row per PHU × Gender × Age cell, yielding a tidy stratum-level table that cleanly supports both descriptive stratified analysis and, for ML, weighted case/control expansion.

13 Prepare Tidymodels-Ready Person-Level Frame

vaxed <- uptake |>
  transmute(public_health_unit, gender, age_group,
            weight = n_vaccinated, vaccinated = factor("Yes",
                                                       levels = c("No","Yes"))) |>
  uncount(weight)

unvaxed <- uptake |>
  transmute(public_health_unit, gender, age_group,
            weight = n_unvaccinated, vaccinated = factor("No",
                                                        levels = c("No","Yes"))) |>
  uncount(weight)

ml_data <- bind_rows(vaxed, unvaxed)

dim(ml_data)
## [1] 15365840        4
tabyl(ml_data, vaccinated)
  • Of the total population counts represented in the dataset, approximately 13.6% were classified as having received at least one vaccine dose, while 86.4% remained unvaccinated. This imbalance reflects population‑level denominators and underscores the importance of rate‑based analyses rather than raw counts.

14 CONCLUSION

This data wrangling and descriptive analysis prepared the vaccination dataset for valid population‑based inference. Systematic cleaning addressed missing population denominators, clarified categorical structures through defined factor levels and reference groups, and transformed weekly surveillance records into interpretable person‑level summaries. Descriptive results highlighted substantial variation across time and demographic strata, reflecting phased vaccine rollout and uptake patterns. Overall, these steps ensured internal consistency, prevented double‑counting, and produced reliable coverage measures, providing a strong foundation for more advanced analysis in future parts of the project.
